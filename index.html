Decision Making Under Uncertainty Explorer

SciTools bidding, winning probabilities, EMV, and sensitivity analysis
Core idea

A good decision under uncertainty is not the same as a guaranteed good outcome. This explorer helps students see why the EMV-maximizing bid can be rational even when a single simulated contract may lose money.

1

The business situation

SciTools can bid on a government contract. Preparing a bid costs $5,000. Supplying the instruments costs $95,000. If SciTools wins with bid B, profit is B − $100,000. If it loses after bidding, it loses only the $5,000 preparation cost.

Teaching prompt

Ask students: why can the $125K bid become best when no competition is more likely?

Because a higher bid gives more profit if SciTools wins, and a higher probability of no competitor makes winning less dependent on underbidding competitors.

7

Quick check questions

Concept check 1

If SciTools bids $120K and the competitor bids $118K, who wins?

Answer: Competitor wins, because $118K is lower.

Concept check 2

If SciTools bids $115K, can it still lose money?

Answer: Yes. It loses $5K whenever a competitor submits a lower bid.

Concept check 3

Does EMV mean SciTools will earn exactly that amount?

Answer: No. EMV is a weighted average over possible outcomes.
